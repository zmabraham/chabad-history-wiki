# A Prince in Prison

**Subtitle:** The Previous Lubavitcher Rebbe's Account of His Incarceration in Stalinist Russia in 1927

**Author:** Rabbi Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn (Frierdiker Rebbe / Rayatz)

**Source:** Extract from Likkutei Dibburim

**Publisher:** Sichos In English, Brooklyn, NY

**Year:** 1997 (57th Anniversary Edition)

**ISBN:** 1-8814-0027-1

---

## Overview

This document is the Frierdiker Rebbe's first-person account of his arrest, imprisonment, and liberation by Soviet authorities in 1927. It was originally written as *Reshimas HaMaasar* ("An Account of the Imprisonment") and completed in Riga on 15 Sivan, 5688 (1928) — the first anniversary of his arrest.

The work appears as part of Volume IV of *Likkutei Dibburim* and documents the Rebbe's resistance to Stalin's ideological suppression of Jewish life, for which he "spearheaded the underground Jewish resistance" and paid with "brutal incarceration and a capital sentence."

---

## Structure of the Work

The published volume contains four main parts plus an appendix:

### Part I: Sections 1-17 — The Imprisonment of 1927
Details the arrest from June 15, 1927 (15 Sivan 5687) at 2:15 AM through the imprisonment period. Describes:
- Midnight arrest by GPU agents in Leningrad
- The search of the Rebbe's home
- Interrogation and prison conditions
- Divine perspective on suffering

### Part II: Sections 1-20 — The Imprisonment of 1927
Continuation of the imprisonment narrative

### Part III: Sections 1-15 — The Imprisonment of 1927
Further details, followed by Sections 16-26: Chronicles of the First Three Generations of Chabad Chassidism

### Part IV: Sections 1-25 — Recollections of Childhood
The Rebbe's memories between ages 6-11, being groomed by his father (Rashab) to succeed him as Rebbe

### Appendix: Prisons are Transient
The Rebbe's farewell message at the train station, 3 Tammuz 5687, upon departure to exile in Kostroma

## Key Events Documented

### Arrest Timeline (1927)
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 15 Sivan (June 14) | Arrest at 2:15 AM in Leningrad |
| 15 Sivan - 3 Tammuz | 18 days, 11 hours, 15 minutes of imprisonment |
| 3 Tammuz, 1:30 PM | Capital charge quashed; sentenced to exile instead |
| 3 Tammuz, 7:30 PM | Departure by train to Kostroma |
| 4 Tammuz | Arrival in Kostroma |
| 12-13 Tammuz | Notification of liberation; actual release |

### Seven Terms of Imprisonment
The Rebbe documents that the 1927 imprisonment was his **seventh**:

1. **First (1892)**: Lubavitch, age 11 — recorded in his recollections book
2. **Second (1902)**: Lubavitch, Iyar 5662 — informers from Haskalah school
3. **Third (1906)**: Lubavitch, Teves 5666 — Poalei Tzion uprising
4. **Fourth (1910)**: Petrograd, Teves 5670 — informer: educated Jew "K"
5. **Fifth (1916)**: Petrograd, Shvat 5676 — military exemption advocacy
6. **Sixth (1920)**: Rostov on Don, Tammuz 5680 — denounced by Yevsektsia head "D"
7. **Seventh (1927)**: Leningrad — capital sentence, commuted to exile, then liberation

## Key Themes

### Mesirus Nefesh (Self-Sacrifice)
The Rebbe writes: "A period rich in suffering which is inflicted on account of his vigorous endeavors for an ideal... escalates a man's personality and gifts. Such days are the luminous days in a man's life."

### Divine Perspective
"Normally, an analogy is less earnest than its analog. Consider, then: If imprisoning a body in a jail of wood and stone is called suffering, then how intense must be the suffering of the Divine soul when it is imprisoned in the body and the animal soul."

### Transience vs. Eternity
From the farewell message: "We must always bear in mind that prisons and hard-labor camps are transient, whereas the Torah, its mitzvos, and the Jewish people, are eternal."

## Childhood Recollections (Part IV)

The fourth section contains vivid memories of the young Yosef Yitzchak (ages 6-11):

- **Education**: Relationship with teacher R. Shneur Slonim
- **Family dynamics**: Learning about his grandfather (Maharash)'s instruction that his sons never engage in business, only Torah study
- **First Yom Tov experiences**: Staying awake for Shavuos Tikkun
- **Trips to Mozinkes**: Accompanying his father to the forest resort
- **Kharkov farbrengen**: Witnessing his father's farbrengen as a young child, being passed from chassid to chassid
- **Emotional sensitivity**: Deep connection to his mother's feelings

## Historical Context

### The GPU/NKVD
The document provides contemporary witness to the Stalinist secret police operations:
- Midnight arrests without warrants
- Interrogation tactics and intimidation
- Prison conditions and humiliation
- Use of informers within the Jewish community (Yevsektsia)

### Jewish Underground Resistance
The publisher's foreword notes that the Rebbe "spearheaded the underground Jewish resistance to Stalin's ideological Final Solution" — referring to the systematic suppression of Jewish religious life.

## Companion Volume

**Defiance and Devotion** (Kehot, 1996) is described as an "invaluable companion" — it contains selected discourses (maamarim) delivered by the Rebbe Rayatz during the arrest and liberation period, calling on chassidim to defy the Soviet regime "even at the cost of literal self-sacrifice."

## Notable Quotes

### On the nature of suffering:
"Every single incident in such a period is significant... For every hour and minute of torment gives rise to inestimable benefits: it makes a man so resolute that even a weakling is transformed into the most courageous of men."

### On his arrest:
"I answered coolly and clearly: 'I don't know which Schneersohn you are looking for... As to the officers of the said administration, I have not feared them, I do not fear them, and I shall not fear them.'"

### Farewell message (3 Tammuz 1927):
"Nevertheless, all the nations on the face of the earth must know: Our bodies alone were banished into exile to be ruled by the nations of the world. Our souls were never exiled, nor were they subjected to the rule of the nations."
