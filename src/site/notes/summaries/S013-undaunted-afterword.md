# Undaunted - Afterword: A Maamar in Brooklyn

## Overview

The Afterword covers the first yahrzeit of the Rayatz in 1951 and the dramatic moment when the Ramash (Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson) accepted leadership to become the seventh Lubavitcher Rebbe.

## The Context

### First Yahrzeit (10 Shevat 1951)
- **Date:** One year after Rayatz's passing (January 1950)
- **Location:** 770 Eastern Parkway, Brooklyn
- **Gathering:** Chasidim flocked for farbrengen
- **Question on everyone's mind:** Would the Ramash become the next Rebbe?

### Leadership Uncertainty
Unlike the Rashab who left clear instructions for his son to succeed him, the Rayatz left no directive. In the immediate wake of his passing:
- **Some** supported eldest son-in-law Rabbi Shmaryahu Gurary
- **Many** gravitated toward the Ramash
- **Time** revealed the Ramash as the clear choice

## The Events Leading to Acceptance

### Letters and Pledges
For twelve months, Chasidim worldwide sent letters urging the Ramash to become Rebbe, signing a *ktav hitkarut* (pledge of loyalty).

### Private Meeting
A few weeks before the yahrzeit, distinguished New York Chasidim met with the Ramash and presented a letter signed by Chasidim throughout the US declaring him the new Rebbe.

**The Ramash's reaction:** When he read the first line, he broke down in tears, saying "This has no connection to me."

### Wife's Influence
According to chasidic tradition, it was his wife who propelled him to his final decision, imploring: "If you don't become Rebbe, the work of my father will have been for naught."

## The Farbrengen (10 Shevat 1951)

### The Scene
- **Time:** 9:45 PM
- **Location:** Small synagogue in 770
- **Crowd:** "Packed to the rafters"

### The Ramash Speaks
He began by speaking about strengthening the bond between Rebbe and Chasidim: "Each of us must continue the task the Rebbe entrusted us with."

He explored *ahavat Yisrael* — the responsibility to love every Jew, the central mitzvah.

### The Dramatic Moment
The crowd grew restless. Would the Ramash say a maamar as his predecessors did when becoming Rebbe?

Suddenly, **Rabbi Sender Nemtzov** — over eighty years old, former student in Lubavitch yeshivah, rabbi in Manchester for many years — rose and proclaimed: "We want the Rebbe to say chasidut [a maamar]!"

**Absolute silence** filled the room as all eyes turned to the Ramash.

### Acceptance
To everyone's delight, the new Rebbe began his first maamar. He started by referencing the last maamar the Rayatz had published on the eve of his passing:

> "My father-in-law, the Rebbe, of blessed memory, writes as follows in the maamar that he released for the day of his passing, the tenth of Shevat 5710 (1950), [based on the verse in Song of Songs]: 'I have come into my garden, my sister, my bride.'"

### The Celebration
After the first part of the maamar, the Ramash asked everyone to say lechaim. Rabbi Nemtzov — despite his advanced age — leaped onto a table and called out: "Chasidim, we have a Rebbe!" before proclaiming Shehechiyanu.

## The Continuity

### The Motif
This became the motif of the seventh Rebbe's leadership: building on the foundation established by the Rayatz, expanding Chabad into the largest Jewish movement in the world.

Time and again, he referred to the teachings and directives of his father-in-law as his guiding light.

### The Legacy
Forty-two years later in 1994, when the seventh Rebbe passed away, he was interred alongside his father-in-law at the Ohel.

Today, hundreds of thousands flock to pray at this holy site every year. The two graves are enveloped by stone walls that rise up to the open sky. Two simple granite headstones mark the resting place of the two great leaders of modern Jewish life who transformed Jewish destiny and created a spiritual renaissance that continues to reach every community in the world.

## Historical Significance

This moment marked:
- Transition from sixth to seventh Rebbe
- Beginning of global Chabad expansion
- Continuity of spiritual leadership
- Foundation for modern Jewish outreach movement
