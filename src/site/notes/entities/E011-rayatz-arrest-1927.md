# 1927 Arrest of the Frierdiker Rebbe

The arrest and imprisonment of Rabbi Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn (the Frierdiker Rebbe / Rayatz) by Soviet authorities in 1927, culminating in his liberation on Yud-Beis Tammuz.

## The Arrest

From the Rebbe's account in [[S017-a-prince-in-prison]]:

> "A few minutes after midnight, weary and fatigued, I washed my hands in preparation for the evening meal together with my family. Before ten minutes had passed the doorbell rang noisily. The door opened and two men rushed into the dining room, shouting: 'We were sent by the administration of the GPU. Who is Schneersohn? Where is he?'"
>
> "Within a moment a unit of armed men entered, stood in line, and awaited orders."

### The Rebbe's Response

When asked to show the agents through his home:

> "I answered coolly and clearly: 'I don't know which Schneersohn you are looking for... As to the officers of the said administration, I have not feared them, I do not fear them, and I shall not fear them.'"

---

## Charges and Sentencing

The Rebbe was initially sentenced to death for "counter-revolutionary activities" — primarily his work maintaining Jewish religious life under Soviet rule.

The sentence was quashed on 3 Tammuz and commuted to three years of exile in Kostroma, a remote city on the Volga River.

---

## Liberation

The famous liberation on **Yud-Beis Tammuz** (12 Tammuz) — which became a Chabad holiday celebrating the Rebbe's release — came less than two weeks after his departure to exile.

---

## Historical Significance

### Seventh Imprisonment
This was the Rebbe's **seventh** imprisonment:

1. 1892 (age 11) — Lubavitch
2. 1902 — Lubavitch
3. 1906 — Lubavitch
4. 1910 — Petrograd
5. 1916 — Petrograd
6. 1920 — Rostov on Don
7. **1927 — Leningrad** (the only one with capital sentence)

### Underground Resistance
The arrest was part of the Soviet campaign against Jewish religious life. The Rebbe had "spearheaded the underground Jewish resistance to Stalin's ideological Final Solution."

### Defiance and Devotion
During this period, the Rebbe delivered maamarim (discourses) calling on chassidim to maintain Jewish life "even at the cost of literal self-sacrifice." These were later published in *Defiance and Devotion* (Kehot, 1996).

---

## Farewell Message (3 Tammuz 1927)

Standing on the train step before departure to exile, the Rebbe addressed the chassidim:

> "We must openly declare for all to hear, that with regard to everything involving our religion — the Torah of the people of Israel, with its commandments and customs — no one is going to impose his views on us, and no force has the right to subjugate us."
>
> "We must always bear in mind that prisons and hard-labor camps are transient, whereas the Torah, its mitzvos, and the Jewish people, are eternal."
