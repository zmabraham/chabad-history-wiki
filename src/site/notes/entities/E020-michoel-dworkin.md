# Reb Michoel Dworkin

Lubavitcher chassid known for his extraordinary mesirus nefesh (self-sacrifice) during the Rebbe's 1927 imprisonment and exile.

## Actions During 1927 Crisis

### Journey to Kostroma

When word came that the Rebbe would be exiled to **Kostroma** on the Volga River:
- **Left immediately** for Kostroma before the Rebbe's arrival
- **Arrived earlier** than the Rebbe to prepare for his stay
- **Gathered children** and established a **cheder** (underground religious school)
- **Fixed the mikveh** (ritual bath) for the Rebbe's use

### The Liberation Scene

When the Rebbe was liberated on **12 Tammuz 5687 (July 12, 1927)**:

> *"Upon his arrival home, the Rebbe viewed an unusual and moving spectacle— the chassid Reb Michoel Dworkin was dancing round the house, a bottle of wine in his hand, and upon his lips a melody with Russian words, singing with great feeling: 'Nyet, nyet nikago' — 'Nothing exists aside from G-d!'"*

The small son of the shochet (ritual slaughterer) in whose home the Rebbe stayed also danced in somersault fashion at this celebration.

## Historical Significance

Reb Michoel Dworkin exemplifies:
1. **Proactive mesirus nefesh** — didn't wait to be told what to do; took initiative
2. **Underground dedication** — continued Jewish education despite Soviet prohibition
3. **Joyous faith** — celebrated the liberation with ecstatic dancing
4. **Chassidic devotion** — placed himself at risk to serve the Rebbe

## Context: Underground Jewish Education in Soviet Russia

Reb Michoel's establishment of a cheder in Kostroma was part of broader underground resistance:

- The **Va'ad** organization (led by the Rebbe) funded education of 5,000 children
- 16,000 more had to be refused for **lack of funds**
- Religious education was limited to **3 pupils per teacher** by Soviet law
- Despite restrictions, yeshivot and chadarim operated underground
